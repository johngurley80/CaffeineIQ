## Quick Answer

**Caffeine has a half-life of approximately 5 hours.** This means 5 hours after consuming 200mg of caffeine, around 100mg remains in your system. It takes roughly 10 hours to reduce to 25% — and up to 24 hours to fall below any detectable level.

---

## The Caffeine Half-Life Explained

Caffeine is metabolised by the liver enzyme CYP1A2. "Half-life" means the time it takes your body to reduce the concentration of caffeine in your bloodstream by 50%.

| Hours after consumption | Caffeine remaining (starting from 200mg) |
|---|---|
| 0 | 200mg |
| 5 | 100mg |
| 10 | 50mg |
| 15 | 25mg |
| 20 | 12.5mg |
| 25 | ~6mg |

Most sleep researchers consider caffeine levels below 25–50mg as unlikely to significantly affect sleep for the average adult. Based on a 200mg dose, that means caffeine stops being meaningfully disruptive around **10–15 hours** after consumption.

This is why a 3pm coffee can still affect your 11pm sleep.

---

## Why Your Half-Life May Be Different

The 5-hour average hides enormous individual variation. Key factors:

**Genetics (CYP1A2 gene)** — The single biggest factor. Fast metabolisers clear caffeine in as little as 2.5–3 hours. Slow metabolisers may take 7–10 hours for the same reduction. You cannot determine this without a genetic test.

**Age** — Caffeine metabolism slows with age. Older adults typically have longer half-lives.

**Pregnancy** — Caffeine half-life extends dramatically during pregnancy, reaching 15 hours or more in the third trimester. This is why the recommendation drops from 400mg to 200mg/day.

**Liver health** — Liver disease significantly extends caffeine half-life.

**Smoking** — Smokers metabolise caffeine roughly twice as fast as non-smokers (CYP1A2 induction).

**Oral contraceptives** — Extend caffeine half-life by approximately 40–65%.

**Certain medications** — Fluconazole, fluvoxamine, and other CYP1A2 inhibitors slow metabolism significantly.

## What This Means for Your Sleep

Most sleep researchers recommend cutting off caffeine **6–8 hours before bedtime** for average metabolisers. But this is a rough average. For fast metabolisers, 4 hours may be sufficient. For slow metabolisers or those on contraceptives, 10 hours may be necessary.

The insidious thing about caffeine and sleep: caffeine doesn't just delay sleep onset. It suppresses deep slow-wave sleep and REM sleep even when it doesn't keep you awake. You may fall asleep fine but wake up unrefreshed.

## How Long Does Caffeine Stay in Urine, Blood, and Saliva?

| Test type | Detection window |
|---|---|
| Blood | 1.5–9.5 hours |
| Saliva | Up to 24 hours |
| Urine | Up to 48 hours (very high doses longer) |

These windows are mainly relevant in clinical or testing contexts. For sleep purposes, blood concentration (affecting the brain) is the relevant measure.

---

## Does Food or Water Speed Up Caffeine Clearance?

No proven way to accelerate caffeine metabolism exists. Water does not flush caffeine faster — the liver metabolises it at a fixed rate determined by your genetics and the factors above.

Common myths that don't work:
- Drinking extra water
- Exercise immediately post-consumption
- Eating food after your coffee

What food and water *do* help with is managing the side effects of caffeine (jitteriness, elevated heart rate) — but the caffeine itself leaves at the same pace regardless.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long does caffeine stay in your system?
Caffeine has a half-life of approximately 5 hours for the average adult. Starting from a 200mg dose, around 25mg remains after 15 hours. Full elimination takes up to 24 hours at normal doses. Individual variation is significant depending on genetics, age, and other factors.

### Does caffeine stay in your system longer if you drink more?
Yes. The half-life remains approximately 5 hours regardless of dose, but the total amount in your system is higher with larger doses — so it takes longer to fall below levels that affect sleep. 400mg takes roughly 2 hours longer to reach the same threshold as 100mg.

### How long after drinking coffee can I go to sleep?
Most sleep researchers recommend stopping caffeine 6–8 hours before bedtime for average metabolisers. Fast metabolisers may be fine with 4–5 hours; slow metabolisers may need 10+ hours. Use the CaffeineIQ tool to calculate your specific cut-off based on your intake and bedtime.

### Does caffeine affect sleep even if I fall asleep easily?
Yes. Caffeine suppresses deep slow-wave sleep and REM sleep even in people who fall asleep without difficulty. This can result in lower sleep quality and next-day fatigue even when sleep duration appears normal.

### Is caffeine still in your system the next morning?
Possibly, yes. If you consumed 400mg of caffeine at 2pm and have a standard 5-hour half-life, roughly 50mg remains at midnight and around 25mg at 5am. For slow metabolisers, the figure is higher. This "sleep debt cycling" — where residual caffeine prevents full recovery each night — is a key reason many people feel they need caffeine to function normally.
